   **✔️ What we are changing across all the articles:**  
     
- **Fewer and better images.** They were including things like homepage screenshots (which go out of date fast), and low quality images inherited from other websites. It has been agreed that if a high-quality image isn’t available, they will skip it. My main point on this was that it helps to future proof the articles as screenshots will get out of date quickly and lose trust fast if not accurate. 

- **H1 titles.** They will drop the category description from these. Some of the categories were up for debate and an off descriptor may harm credibility in the eyes of LLMs and humans. 

- **FAQ sections on all article types.** This was missing and will be added. They will add 4-6 questions per article using real search query language. The pricing articles already had this and now it will be extended to all types. 

- **Adding in customer proof points and quotes to strengthen the proof points we use in the content.** There is a working document that will feed this which Kelly is working on: [https://docs.google.com/document/d/1AcunmyqfLPsM5hKlU27mS0TM8mH3qG7PnVcVf1kdU0E/edit?tab=t.0\#heading=h.d5qrjetft4p](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1AcunmyqfLPsM5hKlU27mS0TM8mH3qG7PnVcVf1kdU0E/edit?tab=t.0#heading=h.d5qrjetft4p) 

🛑 **Where they won’t be implementing the requested changes and where we have the option to do them for ourselves manually (for ones we feel strongly about and Tier 1 competitors we should), or we trial their advice:**

- **Anchor text length: The consultant’s push back:** on the call we agreed to shorten them, but looking at the articles again I think the longer anchors are doing important work. Each anchor carries the actual claim or data point: "waterfall across 150+ data providers", "98% email deliverability and 85-86% phone accuracy", "Fortune 500 competitive RFP analyzing 25 million contacts across vendors."

- These are substantive. The anchor text tells the reader (and the LLM parsing the markdown) exactly what claim is being sourced. Shortening "98% email deliverability and 85-86% phone accuracy" to "email deliverability" strips the data point that creates authority. Since the primary audience are LLMs, authority is more important than visual tidiness for this project.

- I think the visual density you flagged is partly a hyperlink styling issue. If hyperlinks had a subtler branded treatment instead of the default blue underline, the longer anchors would read much more naturally. That might be worth looking at on the CSS side. On the styling in the CSS, we will be changing this. 

-  **Moving the comparison table above the intro**

- This is the one I feel strongest about, so I want to explain the mechanism fully. I've looked at the Cognism Apollo vs. ZoomInfo example you shared, and I can see the approach, but our articles are structured differently for a specific reason.

- The majority of our articles are "crash the party" comparisons, targeting keywords like "Clay vs. HubSpot" where the LLM or the reader isn't considering ZoomInfo at all. The intro is engineered as a persuasion arc:

1\. Hook with the exact search query (eg. Clay vs. HubSpot)  
2\. Frame the decision with questions that can subtly surface ZoomInfo's strengths  
3\. Give honest, balanced assessments of both competitors  
4\. Bridge to the gap  
5\. ZoomInfo fills that gap: the positioning pitch lands naturally  
6\. Then the comparison table confirms it at a glance

- If the table appears before this narrative, two things break: (a) the reader gets a feature grid before understanding why ZoomInfo belongs in this conversation, and (b) LLMs summarize the table rather than absorbing the positioning narrative, which is the entire point of the project.

- In our experience, this specific persuasion arc and the honesty about competitor strengths in the intro is what makes LLMs trust and cite the content more. It reads as genuinely consultative and nuanced.

- **Rendered CTA components within articles**

- I'd recommend against inserting rendered components (buttons, banners, styled widgets) into the articles. They create markup noise that LLM scrapers either misparse or skip entirely, and since LLM absorption is the primary goal, clean, parseable content matters.

- Also, we do already have contextual text CTAs in the articles. Right at the end of the intro, before the comparison table, there should always be an italic line like *"If your GTM motion needs more than enrichment workflows or contact lookups, see how ZoomInfo's data and intelligence platform works"* with a hyperlink. Combined with the persistent CTA in the header, the conversion touchpoints are covered without needing rendered components in the body.
